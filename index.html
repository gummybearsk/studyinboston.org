Visa & Immigration

Everything you need to know about F-1 visa and maintaining status

F-1 Visa Application Process

Step 1: Get Accepted to SEVP-Certified School

Your university must be certified by SEVP (Student and Exchange Visitor Program) to issue Form I-20.

Step 2: Receive Form I-20

Your university will send you Form I-20 after you're accepted and have shown proof of financial support.

Step 3: Pay SEVIS I-901 Fee

Pay the $350 SEVIS fee online at least 3 days before your visa interview.

Maintaining F-1 Status

Academic Requirements

  • Maintain full-time enrollment (minimum 12 credit hours undergraduate, 9 graduate)
  • Make normal progress toward degree
  • Don't exceed 120 days of cumulative unemployment on OPT

Work Authorization

  • On-campus work: Up to 20 hours/week during school, 40 hours during breaks
  • CPT (Curricular Practical Training): For internships/co-ops required for degree
  • OPT (Optional Practical Training): 12 months work authorization after graduation
  • STEM OPT Extension: Additional 24 months for STEM graduates

Important Deadlines

  • Program start: Must enter US no more than 30 days before program start date
  • Grace period: 60 days after program completion to depart or change status
  • Travel: Get travel signature on I-20 if traveling outside US (valid for 1 year)

Emergency Contacts

  • ICE Student Helpline: 1-802-872-6020
  • Your school's DSO (Designated School Official)
  • USCIS National Customer Service Center: 1-800-375-5283

The Ultimate Guide to Boston's Student Neighborhoods

Complete breakdown of where to live as an international student

Introduction

Choosing where to live in Boston as an international student is one of your most important decisions. The right neighborhood can enhance your academic experience, help you build lasting friendships, and keep you within budget. This comprehensive guide breaks down the best areas for students, with detailed analysis of costs, transportation, safety, and lifestyle factors.

Allston-Brighton: The Student Capital

Overview

Allston-Brighton is Boston's unofficial student neighborhood, home to thousands of college students from Boston University, Harvard, and other area schools. This vibrant area offers the best combination of affordable housing, active nightlife, and strong student community.

Housing Costs

  • Shared apartment: $800-1,200/month
  • Studio: $1,600-2,200/month
  • 1-bedroom: $2,000-2,800/month
  • Utilities: $50-100/month

Transportation

Green Line B and D branches serve the area, connecting directly to downtown Boston and Cambridge. The 57 bus runs frequently along Commonwealth Avenue. Travel time to downtown is 20-30 minutes.

Lifestyle & Amenities

Allston offers incredible student nightlife with numerous bars, clubs, and late-night eateries. The area has several grocery stores including Star Market and ethnic food markets. Brighton has more residential feel with families, while Allston proper is more student-focused.

Pros

  • Most affordable student housing in Boston
  • Vibrant nightlife and social scene
  • Large international student population
  • Close to Boston University
  • Many restaurants and convenience stores

Cons

  • Can be noisy, especially on weekends
  • Some buildings are older with maintenance issues
  • Limited parking availability
  • Heavy student turnover in buildings

Somerville: Hip and Artistic

Overview

Somerville has evolved from a working-class neighborhood to Boston's creative hub. Popular with graduate students and young professionals, it offers a more mature atmosphere while still being student-friendly.

Housing Costs

  • Shared apartment: $900-1,400/month
  • Studio: $1,800-2,400/month
  • 1-bedroom: $2,300-3,200/month

Best Areas

  • Davis Square: Red Line access, restaurants, nightlife
  • Porter Square: Quieter, close to Harvard
  • Ball Square: More affordable, emerging area
  • Union Square: Trendy, upcoming Green Line extension

Cambridge: Academic Heaven

Overview

Home to Harvard and MIT, Cambridge offers an intellectual atmosphere and beautiful historic architecture. More expensive but worth it for the academic environment and proximity to world-class universities.

Housing Costs

  • Shared apartment: $1,000-1,600/month
  • Studio: $2,000-2,800/month
  • 1-bedroom: $2,500-3,500/month

Neighborhoods

  • Harvard Square: Expensive but iconic
  • Central Square: More diverse and affordable
  • Kendall Square: Tech hub, near MIT
  • Porter Square: Good value, Red Line access

Conclusion

Your choice of neighborhood will significantly impact your Boston experience. Consider your budget, university location, lifestyle preferences, and career goals when making this important decision. Remember that you can always move after your first year once you better understand the city and your needs.

Top 10 Culture Shocks for International Students in Boston

Prepare for the biggest cultural differences you'll encounter

Introduction

Boston is an incredibly welcoming city for international students, but like any major cultural transition, there will be moments of surprise, confusion, and adjustment. Understanding these common culture shocks in advance can help you navigate your first months with greater confidence and less stress.

1. The American Small Talk Culture

What to Expect

Americans, especially in service industries, will ask "How are you?" or "How's your day?" without necessarily wanting a detailed answer. This casual friendliness can feel overwhelming or insincere at first.

How to Navigate

  • Respond with brief, positive answers: "Good, thanks!" or "Great, how about you?"
  • Don't feel obligated to share personal details
  • Understand this is social courtesy, not necessarily deep interest
  • Practice common responses to feel more comfortable

Boston Twist

Bostonians are known for being more direct and less artificially friendly than other parts of the US. Don't be surprised if interactions feel more business-like than in other American cities.

2. Tipping Culture Everywhere

What to Expect

Tipping is expected in restaurants (18-20%), bars, hair salons, delivery services, and many other situations. The base prices you see don't include service charges.

Tipping Guidelines

  • Restaurants: 18-20% of pre-tax bill
  • Bars: $1-2 per drink or 15-20% of tab
  • Food delivery: 15-20% or minimum $3-5
  • Taxis/Uber: 15-20%
  • Coffee shops: $1 for complex drinks (optional for basic coffee)

When NOT to Tip

  • Fast food restaurants
  • Self-service establishments
  • Retail stores
  • Gas stations

3. The Healthcare System Complexity

What to Expect

Unlike many countries with universal healthcare, the US system is complex, expensive, and requires understanding insurance networks, copays, and referrals.

Key Concepts

  • Insurance networks: You must use "in-network" doctors for lower costs
  • Copays: You pay a set amount ($20-50) for most visits
  • Deductibles: You pay this amount before insurance covers costs
  • Emergency room costs: Can be $500-3,000+ for simple issues

Student Health Center Advantage

Most university health centers provide basic care included in your student fees, making them your best first option for non-emergency issues.

4. Academic Participation Expectations

What to Expect

American classrooms emphasize active participation, debate, and questioning professors. Silence can be interpreted as disengagement rather than respect.

Classroom Culture

  • Students regularly interrupt professors with questions
  • Debate and disagreement are encouraged
  • Participation often counts toward your grade
  • Office hours are expected to be used, not just for problems

How to Adapt

  • Prepare questions before class
  • Visit professors during office hours to build relationships
  • Join study groups to practice discussion skills
  • Remember: asking questions shows engagement, not ignorance

5. Personal Space and Physical Contact Norms

What to Expect

Americans maintain larger personal space bubbles and have specific rules about physical contact, especially between genders and in professional settings.

General Guidelines

  • Personal space: Arm's length in most social situations
  • Greetings: Handshakes or brief hugs between close friends
  • Professional settings: Handshakes only, avoid touching
  • Public transportation: Avoid unnecessary physical contact

Boston Specifics

Bostonians tend to be less touchy-feely than Americans from other regions. Handshakes are standard for business; hugs are reserved for close friendships.

Remember, culture shock is a sign that you're growing and adapting to a new environment. These challenges will become sources of strength and interesting stories as you become more comfortable in your new Boston home.

Understanding the American Academic System

GPA System

  • 4.0 Scale: A=4.0, B=3.0, C=2.0, D=1.0, F=0.0
  • Letter Grade Ranges: A (90-100%), B (80-89%), C (70-79%), D (60-69%), F (<60%)
  • Plus/Minus System: B+ = 3.3, B = 3.0, B- = 2.7
  • Minimum GPA: Most programs require 2.0+ to stay enrolled

Course Structure

  • Credit Hours: Most courses are 3-4 credits
  • Full-time Status: 12+ credits undergraduate, 9+ graduate
  • Class Participation: Often 10-20% of final grade
  • Attendance: May be required, affects grades

Working in Boston as an International Student: Rules, Tips, and Best Jobs

Complete guide to earning money legally while maintaining your F-1 status

Understanding F-1 Visa Work Regulations

As an F-1 student, you have specific work authorization rules that you must follow to maintain your legal status. Violating these rules can result in deportation and prevent future US visa applications. However, there are several legal ways to earn money while studying.

Work Hour Limitations

  • During academic year: Maximum 20 hours per week
  • During breaks (summer, winter): Full-time (40 hours per week) allowed
  • Between semesters: Full-time if you're enrolled for next semester
  • Final semester: Can work full-time if course load is reduced for graduation

On-Campus Employment (No Authorization Needed)

On-campus work is the easiest way for F-1 students to earn money legally. You can start immediately without any additional paperwork beyond your F-1 status.

Definition of "On-Campus"

  • Work performed on university premises
  • Work for university-affiliated organizations
  • Work that provides services to students on campus
  • Some off-campus locations if they're educationally affiliated

CPT (Curricular Practical Training)

CPT allows you to work off-campus in jobs related to your field of study. This must be a required part of your curriculum or count for academic credit.

CPT Requirements

  • Must be authorized by your school's DSO (Designated School Official)
  • Job must be related to your major field of study
  • Must have completed at least one academic year (except for graduate students)
  • Work must be part of your curriculum or count for credit

CPT Limitations

  • Part-time CPT: 20 hours or less per week
  • Full-time CPT: More than 20 hours per week
  • Important: 12+ months of full-time CPT makes you ineligible for OPT

OPT (Optional Practical Training)

OPT allows you to work in your field of study for 12 months after graduation (24 additional months for STEM fields).

Types of OPT

  • Pre-completion OPT: Before graduation (rare, part-time only during school)
  • Post-completion OPT: After graduation (most common)
  • STEM OPT Extension: Additional 24 months for STEM graduates

OPT Application Process

  1. Meet with your international student advisor
  2. Get I-20 recommendation from DSO
  3. File Form I-765 with USCIS
  4. Pay $410 filing fee
  5. Wait for Employment Authorization Document (EAD)
